    My first programming language was R, which I learned the summer before my final year for my econometrics dissertation. I loved making visualizations using ggplot2 and spending hours learning new LaTeX formatting techniques. I knew that after university, I wanted a role where I would program for the majority of my day. For an economics graduate, data science made the most sense. In the first few years of my career, I relied mainly on Python and SQL.

    A highlight of my time in data science was when I was working at a newspaper and took part in a winning team for BBC's 2020 News Hack. Our proposed "source" index for articles (rather than traditional topic or author indexing) provided a new dimension with which to explore the paper's content. This would not only allow readers to search through articles by the people interviewed e.g. quotes from prospective homebuyers in the Midwest or comments from sociologists based out of NYC, it would also allow for audits of news source diversity. Read more here.

    Eventually, I gravitated from data science to engineering because I liked the idea of building my own things. Right now, I am working on the backend team at a cloud storage company, using primarily Node.js and Typescript while dabbling in infrastructure. Recently, I started doing frontend web development on the side. I coded this website by hand with just HTML, CSS and Javascript.
